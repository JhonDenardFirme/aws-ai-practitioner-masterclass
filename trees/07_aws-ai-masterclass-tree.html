The AI Governance Board │ │ ├─── Legal — Represents regulatory and contractual risk │ │ ├─── Compliance — Ensures alignment with industry and legal standards │ │ ├─── Data Privacy — Represents personal and regulated data concerns │ │ ├─── AI Experts — Bring technical understanding of model behavior and limitations │ │ └─── Business Stakeholders — Represent organizational value and risk tolerance │ ├─── C2. Governance Responsibilities │ │ ├─── Risk Oversight — Ongoing identification and management of AI-related risk │ │ ├─── Policy Creation — Writing the rules that govern data, model, and deployment lifecycle │ │ ├─── Ethical Review — Evaluating AI decisions and use cases against ethical standards │ │ ├─── Compliance Management — Ensuring policies actually satisfy external regulatory requirements │ │ └─── Decision-Making Processes — Defining who approves what, and under what criteria │ └─── C3. Common Compliance Standards │ ├─── NIST — U.S. federal security framework │ ├─── NIST — Focus: Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability │ ├─── ENISA — EU cybersecurity guidance │ ├─── ENISA — Focus: digital trust, cybersecurity certification │ ├─── ISO — International security management standards, e.g. ISO/IEC 27002 │ ├─── SOC Reports — Third-party audits of AWS controls, validating operational compliance │ ├─── HIPAA — Protects healthcare information — applies to Protected Health Information (PHI) │ ├─── GDPR — EU privacy regulation protecting personal data and privacy rights │ └─── PCI DSS — Payment card security standard protecting financial transaction data ├─── E. AI-Specific Governance Challenges │ ├─── E1. Complexity, Dynamism, and Emergence │ │ ├─── Complexity and Opacity — AI systems are difficult to interpret and often behave as black boxes — even their own developers can't always explain a specific output. │ │ ├─── Dynamism and Adaptability — AI models can evolve after deployment, changing behavior over time in ways a governance policy written at launch may not anticipate. │ │ └─── Emergent Capabilities — Unexpected behaviors can emerge that were never explicitly programmed — a governance risk unique to large-scale learned systems. │ ├─── E2. Unique AI Risks │ │ ├─── Algorithmic Bias — Systematic unfairness in outputs, caused by biased training data, embedded human assumptions, or historical discrimination patterns baked into the data. │ │ ├─── Privacy Violations — AI may expose sensitive data; improper handling creates direct compliance failure risk, not just reputational risk. │ │ └─── Misinformation — AI can generate inaccurate content; hallucinations specifically create trust issues that compound over repeated use. │ └─── E3. Algorithm Accountability │ ├─── Accountable AI must be explainable and auditable │ └─── Example laws: EU Artificial Intelligence Act, NYC Automated Decision Systems Law ├─── F. Regulated Workloads │ ├─── F1. What Makes a Workload Regulated │ │ ├─── Auditing — Records and decisions must be independently reviewable │ │ ├─── Data Retention — Data must be archived for a defined legal period │ │ ├─── Compliance Certifications — The workload must meet a named external standard │ │ └─── Legal Oversight — Legal counsel is involved in workload design or review │ └─── F2. Examples and Diagnostic Questions │ ├─── Common regulated industries: financial services, healthcare, aerospace │ └─── Four diagnostic questions determine regulated status ├─── G. Data Protection, Lifecycle, and Responsible AI │ │ ├─── Data Protection and Privacy — Protects sensitive, personal, and regulated information through encryption, access controls, incident response, and breach mitigation. │ │ ├─── Data Lifecycle Management — Governs storage, archiving, retention, and deletion across five stages: collection, processing, storage, consumption, disposal/archiving. │ │ └─── Responsible AI — Promotes fairness, transparency, accountability, and bias mitigation through active bias monitoring, fairness auditing, team education, and consequence analysis. │ └─── H4. Data Analysis and Retention │ └─── Data retention is influenced by regulations, retraining needs, and storage costs ├─── J. Governance Strategy Implementation │ ├─── J1.
