A Foundations of Security, Governance, and Compliance A
A1Security, Governance, and Compliance Defined

Three distinct but complementary functions protect an AI system.

Security
  • Protects Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (the CIA Triad)
Governance
  • Ensures organizational oversight, risk management, business value alignment
Compliance
  • Ensures adherence to laws, regulatory conformity, industry standards
KeySecurity protects, Governance directs, Compliance validates — the module's core distinction›CIA Triad = Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability
A2Why These Functions Matter

These three functions are described as non-negotiable organizational requirements, not optional extras.

Protect Operations
  • Keeps organizational operations running without disruption
Prevent Legal Liabilities
  • Reduces exposure to lawsuits and regulatory penalties
Build Customer Trust
  • Demonstrates the organization handles data and decisions responsibly
Support Responsible AI
  • Provides the operational foundation responsible AI deployment depends on
KeySecurity, Governance, and Compliance are core operational safeguards, not optional add-ons
B Defense in Depth Strategy for AI Systems B
B1What Defense in Depth Is

Defense in Depth is a multi-layered security architecture that uses redundant controls to minimize single points of failure — if one layer is breached, others still stand between the attacker and the system. Generative AI systems process sensitive data, require layered governance, and introduce novel attack surfaces (prompts, embeddings, model APIs) that traditional application security didn't need to account for.

KeyDefense in Depth = multiple security layers working together›Purpose: prevent attacks, detect threats, respond to incidents, recover from failures
B2The Seven Defense Layers

Seven layers, applied together, cover an AI workload end to end — from the data itself up through organizational policy.

1. Data Protection
  • Data at rest: AWS KMS encryption, customer-managed keys, S3 Versioning
2. Identity & Access Management
  • Ensures only authorized entities gain access via AWS IAM
3. Application Protection
  • Protects against unauthorized access, DoS attacks, data breaches
4. Network & Edge Protection
  • Secures network boundaries, traffic flow, cloud perimeter
5. Infrastructure Protection
  • Protects compute resources, cloud infrastructure, availability, and integrity
6. Threat Detection & Incident Response
  • Detection: AWS Security Hub, Amazon GuardDuty
7. Policies, Procedures & Awareness
  • Enforces least privilege access and governance awareness
KeySeven layers: Data, IAM, Application, Network/Edge, Infrastructure, Threat Detection/Response, Policies›Least privilege reduces security risk exposure — the module's most repeated principle
C Governance and Compliance Strategy C
C1The AI Governance Board

Organizations establish AI governance structures, policies, and accountability mechanisms through a cross-functional governance board.

Legal
  • Represents regulatory and contractual risk
Compliance
  • Ensures alignment with industry and legal standards
Data Privacy
  • Represents personal and regulated data concerns
AI Experts
  • Bring technical understanding of model behavior and limitations
Business Stakeholders
  • Represent organizational value and risk tolerance
KeyThe AI Governance Board is cross-functional by design — no single department owns AI governance alone
C2Governance Responsibilities

The board's responsibilities span the full lifecycle of an AI system, not just its initial launch.

Risk Oversight
  • Ongoing identification and management of AI-related risk
Policy Creation
  • Writing the rules that govern data, model, and deployment lifecycle
Ethical Review
  • Evaluating AI decisions and use cases against ethical standards
Compliance Management
  • Ensuring policies actually satisfy external regulatory requirements
Decision-Making Processes
  • Defining who approves what, and under what criteria
KeyPolicies must address the data lifecycle, model lifecycle, monitoring, and incident management
C3Benefits of Governance

Strong governance pays off in two directions at once: it enables growth and prevents damage.

Enables Scalable Deployment
  • Clear policy lets AI expand across the organization without re-litigating risk each time
Enables Business Transformation
  • Confidence in AI systems supports bigger, riskier initiatives
Prevents Ethical Violations
  • Structured review catches problems before they reach production
Prevents Legal Penalties
  • Compliance alignment reduces regulatory exposure
Prevents Reputational Damage
  • Public trust survives incidents better when governance was visibly in place
KeyAI governance builds trust and responsible deployment — the module's key insight for this section

E AI-Specific Governance Challenges E
E1Complexity, Dynamism, and Emergence

Three properties make AI systems structurally harder to govern than traditional software.

Complexity and Opacity

AI systems are difficult to interpret and often behave as black boxes.

Dynamism and Adaptability

AI models can evolve after deployment, changing behavior over time in ways a governance policy written at launch may not anticipate..

Emergent Capabilities

Unexpected behaviors can emerge that were never explicitly programmed.

KeyAI systems can develop unexpected capabilities — the module's key insight for this subsection
E2Unique AI Risks

Three risk categories recur across AI governance discussions, each with a distinct root cause.

Algorithmic Bias

Systematic unfairness in outputs, caused by biased training data, embedded human assumptions, or historical discrimination patterns baked into the data..

Privacy Violations

AI may expose sensitive data; improper handling creates direct compliance failure risk, not just reputational risk..

Misinformation

AI can generate inaccurate content; hallucinations specifically create trust issues that compound over repeated use..

KeyThree unique AI risks: Algorithmic Bias, Privacy Violations, Misinformation
E3Algorithm Accountability

Algorithm Accountability requires transparency, explainability, and human oversight — the goal is ensuring AI decisions are fair and ethical, and that someone can actually answer for them. [+] Two real regulatory examples anchor this requirement: the EU Artificial Intelligence Act and the NYC Automated Decision Systems Law, both of which impose explainability and audit obligations on qualifying AI systems.

KeyAccountable AI must be explainable and auditable›Example laws: EU Artificial Intelligence Act, NYC Automated Decision Systems Law
F Regulated Workloads F
F1What Makes a Workload Regulated

A regulated workload is one requiring special compliance handling.

Auditing
  • Records and decisions must be independently reviewable
Data Retention
  • Data must be archived for a defined legal period
Compliance Certifications
  • The workload must meet a named external standard
Legal Oversight
  • Legal counsel is involved in workload design or review
KeyIf failure has legal, financial, or safety consequences, the workload is likely regulated — the module's core diagnostic rule
F2Examples and Diagnostic Questions

Common regulated industries include financial services, healthcare, and aerospace. Four diagnostic questions help identify a regulated workload: Do records need auditing? Must data be archived? Are predictions considered legal records? Does the workload use restricted data?.

KeyCommon regulated industries: financial services, healthcare, aerospace›Four diagnostic questions determine regulated status

H Data Governance Strategies for AI H
H1What Data Governance Covers

Data Governance is the framework for managing data quality, security, usage, lifecycle, and compliance, covering collection, storage, processing, usage, and deletion end to end.

KeyAI systems are only as trustworthy as their data — the module's key insight for this section
H2Data Quality, Lineage, and Provenance

Three practices establish whether a dataset can actually be trusted, and whether its history can be reconstructed if something goes wrong.

Data Quality and Integrity

Ensures accuracy, completeness, and consistency through data validation, cleansing, lineage tracking, and provenance documentation..

Data Lineage

Tracks the origin, transformation, and movement of data as it moves through a pipeline..

Data Provenance

Identifies a dataset's historical source, usage history, and how it has evolved over time..

KeyLineage explains where data came from and how it changed — the module's key insight for this subsection
H3Data Protection, Lifecycle, and Responsible AI

Three additional practices round out data governance: keeping data safe, controlling its lifecycle, and actively monitoring for fairness.

Data Protection and Privacy

Protects sensitive, personal, and regulated information through encryption, access controls, incident response, and breach mitigation..

Data Lifecycle Management

Governs storage, archiving, retention, and deletion across five stages: collection, processing, storage, consumption, disposal/archiving..

Responsible AI

Promotes fairness, transparency, accountability, and bias mitigation through active bias monitoring, fairness auditing, team education, and consequence analysis..

KeyResponsible AI requires active monitoring, not just initial design — the module's key insight for this subsection
H4Governance Structures, Roles, and Data Sharing

A governance council oversees AI governance strategy, with three roles carrying distinct responsibilities for the data itself.

Data Stewards
  • Manage day-to-day data quality and usage rules
Data Owners
  • Hold accountability for a given dataset's governance
Data Custodians
  • Handle the technical storage and protection of data
KeyThree governance roles: Data Stewards, Data Owners, Data Custodians›Controlled sharing techniques: data federation, data virtualization
I Data Management Concepts I
I1Data Lifecycles and Logging

Data lifecycles manage data from creation through usage, storage, and disposal.

KeyData logging supports monitoring, debugging, and auditing simultaneously
I2Data Residency, Monitoring, and Drift

Three concepts govern where data lives and whether it's still behaving the way it did when the model was trained.

Data Residency

The physical location where data is stored.

Data Monitoring

Continuously tracks data quality, data drift, and anomalies as new data flows through the system..

Data Drift

Occurs when the input data distribution changes over time, gradually invalidating assumptions the model was trained on..

KeyData drift can degrade model performance after deployment — the module's key insight for this subsection
I3Data Analysis and Retention

Data analysis methods include statistical analysis, data visualization, and Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA).

KeyData retention is influenced by regulations, retraining needs, and storage costs
J Governance Strategy Implementation J
J1Policy Areas

AI policies define principles, usage boundaries, and oversight requirements across six recurring areas.

Data Management
  • Rules for how data is collected, used, and retained
Privacy
  • Protections for personal and sensitive information
Bias Mitigation
  • Requirements for detecting and correcting unfair outcomes
Safety
  • Guardrails preventing harmful or dangerous AI behavior
Human Oversight
  • Defines where a human must review or approve AI decisions
Intellectual Property
  • Addresses ownership and licensing of AI-generated content and training data
KeyAI policies must evolve as technology and regulations change — the module's key insight for this section
J2Review Cadence and Participants

Governance reviews recur on a fixed cadence — monthly, quarterly, or bi-annually — and draw participants from across the organization, not just the technical team.

Technical Teams
  • Assess performance, data quality, and robustness
Legal Teams
  • Assess regulatory and contractual exposure
Compliance Officers
  • Assess adherence to named external standards
End-Users
  • Provide practical, ground-level feedback on system behavior
SMEs
  • Provide domain expertise the technical team may lack
KeyReviews split into Technical Reviews (performance, data quality, robustness) and Non-Technical Reviews (ethics, compliance, Responsible AI alignment)
J3Transparency Standards and Team Training

Organizations should disclose model capabilities, limitations, intended use, and training information as a matter of standard transparency practice, not just when compelled by regulation. Teams require ongoing training in Responsible AI, bias mitigation, governance policies, and regulatory updates — governance is treated as a continuously maintained skill, not a one-time onboarding item.

KeyResponsible AI is both a technical and organizational responsibility — the module's key insight for this subsection
K Monitoring AI Systems K
K1Performance Metrics

Five metrics quantify how well a model is actually performing, and they don't all move together — a model can score well on one while failing another.

Model Accuracy
  • Percentage of correct predictions
Precision
  • True positives ÷ predicted positives
Recall
  • True positives ÷ actual positives
F1-Score
  • Harmonic mean of precision and recall
Latency
  • Time required to generate a prediction
KeyHigh accuracy does not always mean high fairness or low latency — the module's key insight for this section
K2Infrastructure, Fairness, and Compliance Monitoring

Beyond model performance, three additional monitoring categories confirm the system is healthy and fair in production.

Infrastructure Monitoring

Tracks CPU, GPU, memory, storage, and network performance to catch resource-level problems before they cause outages..

Bias and Fairness Monitoring

Detects unfair outcomes and discriminatory patterns emerging after deployment, not just at initial testing..

Compliance Monitoring

Ensures ongoing adherence to laws, Responsible AI standards, and governance frameworks.

KeyMonitoring purpose: reliability, accuracy, compliance, fairness — all four, continuously
L Generative AI Security Scoping Matrix L
L1The Five Scopes

The Scoping Matrix helps classify ownership, control, and security responsibilities.

Scope 1 — Consumer App
  • Public AI services with no model ownership
Scope 2 — Enterprise App
  • Third-party enterprise software with AI features
Scope 3 — Pre-Trained Models
  • Business builds applications using external FM APIs
Scope 4 — Fine-Tuned Models
  • Existing FM customized with business data
Scope 5 — Self-Trained Models
  • Organization fully owns data, training, and architecture
KeyMore model ownership = more security and governance responsibility — the module's core rule for this section
L2Security Disciplines

Four security disciplines apply across every scope, though their weight shifts as ownership increases.

Governance and Compliance
  • Policies, reporting, oversight
Legal and Privacy
  • Data ownership, regulatory obligations, data sharing concerns
Risk Management
  • Threat identification, mitigation planning
Controls and Resilience
  • Security controls, availability, SLA reliability
KeyFour security disciplines apply across all five scopes: Governance & Compliance, Legal & Privacy, Risk Management, Controls & Resilience
M Security and Privacy Considerations for AI M
M1Threat Detection, Vulnerability Management, and Infrastructure Protection

Three practices work together to keep an AI system's attack surface covered, from detecting active attacks to hardening the infrastructure underneath.

Threat Detection

Detects attacks, suspicious behavior, and adversarial activities, including fake content generation, automated attacks, and data manipulation..

Vulnerability Management

Includes penetration testing, code review, and patch management to close weaknesses before they're exploited..

Infrastructure Protection

Protects cloud systems, storage, edge devices, and compute infrastructure the AI workload runs on..

KeyThree practices: Threat Detection, Vulnerability Management, Infrastructure Protection
M2Prompt Injection and Data Encryption

Prompt Injection occurs when malicious prompts manipulate model behavior. Data encryption protects data at rest and in transit; the key requirement underneath any encryption scheme is strong encryption key management — encryption is only as good as how well the keys are protected.

KeyPrompt injection is one of the most critical LLM security risks — the module's key insight for this subsection
N OWASP Top 10 for LLMs N
N1The Ten Risks

OWASP's Top 10 for LLMs is a major industry security framework, naming the ten risk categories that recur across LLM-powered applications.

Prompt Injection
  • Malicious input manipulates model behavior or bypasses safeguards
Insecure Output Handling
  • Model output trusted and used downstream without validation
Training Data Poisoning
  • Malicious data corrupts the model during training
Model Denial of Service
  • Resource-exhaustion attacks against the model itself
Supply Chain Vulnerabilities
  • Risk introduced through third-party models, data, or plugins
Sensitive Information Disclosure
  • Model exposes confidential data in its output
Insecure Plugin Design
  • Poorly secured plugins expand the attack surface
Excessive Agency
  • Giving AI too much autonomy to take consequential action
Overreliance
  • Blind trust in AI outputs without verification
Model Theft
  • Unauthorized copying or extraction of the model itself
KeyOWASP Top 10 for LLMs is a major industry security framework
N2Key Concepts to Distinguish

Three OWASP risks are frequently confused with each other, so it's worth defining them side by side.

Excessive Agency

Giving an AI system too much autonomy to take real-world action without adequate human checkpoints..

Overreliance

Blind trust in AI outputs.

Model Theft

Unauthorized copying or extraction of a proprietary model, exposing its weights or architecture..

KeyExcessive Agency = too much autonomy; Overreliance = too much blind trust; Model Theft = unauthorized model extraction

Q Best Practices for Secure Data Engineering Q
Q1Generative AI Data Types and the Engineering Lifecycle

Three distinct data types flow through a Gen AI system, and each moves through the same six-stage engineering lifecycle: collection, preparation, quality checks, analysis, deployment, monitoring.

User Data
  • User prompts and interactions captured during live use

Q2Secure Data Engineering Practices

Four practices keep the data pipeline itself secure — because a failure here cascades directly into model failure.

Assessing Data Quality

Checks completeness, accuracy, timeliness, and consistency before data is trusted for training or retrieval..

Privacy-Enhancing Technologies

Encryption, tokenization, differential privacy, and data masking reduce exposure of sensitive information..

Data Access Control

MFA, SSO, RBAC, and least privilege limit who can touch the data at all..

Data Integrity

Validation checks, backup strategies, audit trails, and atomicity controls confirm the data hasn't been silently corrupted..

KeyData engineering failures can cascade into model failures — the module's key insight for this section
Module Summary
  • Security protects, Governance directs, and Compliance validates — three distinct functions that together make AI trustworthy, built on AWS's seven-layer Defense in Depth model.
  • AI governance requires a cross-functional board (Legal, Compliance, Data Privacy, AI Experts, Business Stakeholders) and delivers measurable benefits: scalable deployment, risk reduction, and public trust.
  • AI systems create unique governance challenges — opacity, dynamism, emergent capabilities — and unique risks: algorithmic bias, privacy violations, and misinformation, all requiring algorithm accountability.
  • The Generative AI Security Scoping Matrix (Scopes 1–5) shows that security and governance responsibility scale directly with how much of the model an organization owns.
  • Prompt injection and the OWASP Top 10 for LLMs define AI-specific security risk, while the Shared Responsibility Model splits obligations between AWS (security OF the cloud) and the customer (security IN the cloud); secure data engineering and model cards make the whole system auditable.
